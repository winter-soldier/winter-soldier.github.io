What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering is a type of hacking that involves manipulating people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. Social engineers use various techniques to obtain this information, such as phishing emails and phone calls.

Social engineers often use common tactics to trick their victims into revealing sensitive data or performing unauthorized tasks. Some examples include:

  • Pretending to be someone else in order to gain access to private information (e.g., pretending you're from IT support)
  • Sending emails asking for passwords or other sensitive data (e.g., "I need your password so I can fix this issue")

How Social Engineering Impacts Businesses

Social engineering is a risk to businesses. It can lead to financial loss, reputation damage and even legal action.

Social engineers use a variety of methods to gain access to information they shouldn't have access to. The most common method is phishing emails; these are emails that appear legitimate but contain malicious attachments or links that allow hackers into your network. Other popular techniques include impersonating an employee over the phone or in person (known as vishing or smishing) or tricking someone into giving away their password by posing as IT staff member asking for it for "security reasons".

Preventing Social Engineering Attacks

  • Awareness Training.
  • Strong Security Protocols.
  • Monitor Network Activity.

Identifying Social Engineering Attacks

Social engineering attacks can be difficult to identify because they're often carried out by people who appear to be legitimate. Here are some common red flags:

  • Suspicious Activity: If you receive an email from someone asking for your password or account information, it's likely a scam. Don't give out any personal details over email unless you initiated the communication and know who is on the other end of it.
  • Unusual Requests: If someone asks you for something that seems strange or out of character for them (for example, if they say they need access to your computer), take time before responding--and verify that their request is legitimate with someone else in your company who knows them well enough to confirm whether it makes sense for them to ask such things of others within their department or team.

Responding to a Social Engineering Attack

You should take the following steps immediately:

  • Notify appropriate personnel.
  • Document everything.

Recovering from a Social Engineering Attack

Once you've recovered from a social engineering attack, it's important to take steps to prevent future incidents.

  • Remediate the Damage: The first step is to determine how much damage has been done and what needs to be fixed. For example, if an attacker gained access to sensitive data or other company assets, those assets may need to be replaced or repaired before they can be used again.
  • Re-educate Employees: After the remediation process has been completed, it's time for employees who were involved in the incident (or who could have prevented it) receive additional training about how social engineering works so that they can better recognize similar attacks in the future.
  • Implement New Security Measures: Finally, once you've re-educated your staff members on how these types of attacks work and implemented new security measures such as two-factor authentication across all devices used by employees at all times--and especially when accessing sensitive data--you'll be well equipped against any future attempts at social engineering!

Creating a Social Engineering Policy

First, you need to identify your goals and set expectations. This can be done by creating a social engineering policy that outlines the consequences of violating the policy. For example, if someone violates your company's social engineering policy by sending out an email on behalf of another employee without their permission, they may be subject to disciplinary action or termination.

The next step is outlining how employees should handle requests from customers or vendors who want access to sensitive information or systems (i.e., "What are the steps that I need to take in order for this person/company/organization?").

Enforcing Your Social Engineering Policy

Enforcing your social engineering policy is an important step in protecting your organization and its data. The first thing to do is to regularly review and update the policy, as well as monitor compliance. This can be done by setting up monitoring tools that send alerts when someone violates the rules of your organization's social engineering policy.

If you have employees who work remotely or travel frequently, make sure they understand what types of information are sensitive and should not be shared outside of secure networks or devices. If possible, provide training for these workers so they know how best protect themselves against cyber criminals trying to gain access through phishing attacks or other methods such as vishing (voice phishing) where scammers call potential victims posing as someone from IT support asking for passwords over phone calls.
